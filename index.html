The Case for Beneficials

Why Beneficial Insects?

Pests are a natural part of a plant's life and play a role in its micro-ecosystem. Rather than trying to eliminate them once and for all, think of beneficial insects as just another part of regular plant care — the same way you fertilize on a schedule, you can maintain beneficials on a schedule.

⚠ What NOT to use

  • Pesticide sprays kill beneficial insects and allow pests to develop resistance
  • Systemic granules increase spider mite fertility and cause population explosions
  • Neem, Captain Jack's, and "organic" sprays are harmful to beneficials and should be stopped at least two weeks before release

🎯 Targeted by Life Stage

Different predators attack different life stages of a pest. A comprehensive treatment covers adults, larvae, and soil-dwelling pupae simultaneously — something no single spray achieves.

🛡 No Resistance, Ever

Pests cannot develop resistance to being eaten. Predator-prey relationships have been in equilibrium for millions of years — unlike chemical approaches, beneficial insects don't become less effective over time.

🐾 Safe for Pets & People

All beneficials are safe around curious pets and children. They stay where their prey is — on your plants — and will not establish elsewhere in your home.

🌱 Soil Health Bonus

Soil-dwelling beneficials like stratiolaelaps also consume pathogenic fungi and bacteria, actively improving the microbial environment your roots depend on.

The Threats

Common Houseplant Pests

Thrips

Tiny, fast-moving insects that rasp leaf tissue, leaving silvery streaking. Multiple life stages make them challenging — adults, larvae, and soil-dwelling pupae all require different predators.

Multi-stage · Foliage + Soil

Spider Mites

Nearly invisible mites that feed on cell contents, leaving stippling and fine webbing. Highly resistant to most pesticide sprays — pyrethrins can actually cause population flares.

Resistant to pesticides

Mealybugs

Fuzzy, cottony white insects that cluster in leaf joints. Cause yellowing, overall plant decline, and leaf drop. Can feel nearly impossible to eliminate by hand alone.

Foliage · Stems · Roots

Fungus Gnats

Flying adults are annoying; it's the larvae in the soil that cause real damage to roots. Very common in overwatered soil and easy to eliminate once you target the right life stage.

Soil-dwelling larvae

Scale

Insects that hide under a waxy dome on stems and leaves. No pesticide or predator can penetrate the dome itself — treatment targets juveniles streaming out underneath it.

Stems · Foliage

Broad & Flat Mites

Microscopic mites invisible to the naked eye. Broad mites frequently attack hoya and cannabis; damage appears as distorted, hardened new growth or bronzing at growing tips.

New growth distortion

The Allies

Beneficial Insects & Mites

Lacewing Larvae

Generalist "aphid lions." Short 10–14 day indoor life cycle; require recurring application. Can be released alongside other beneficials (release first, then wait 15 minutes).

ThripsAphidsMealybugsSpider Mite EggsScale (juvenile)

Minute Pirate Bug

The best thrips predator. Attacks all mobile thrips stages, consuming up to 30 per day. Also hunts for sport — a genuinely voracious predator. More expensive than lacewings.

Thrips (all stages)Whitefly

Cryptolaemus Larvae

The "mealybug destroyer." Young larvae eat 250+ mealybug nymphs or 1,000+ eggs during their larval stage. Order larvae, not adult beetles — adults suit greenhouse scale.

Mealybugs (all species)Some scale

Cucumeris Mite

Industry standard for thrips. Senses where larvae emerge from leaves and attacks them as they hatch. Available as bottle (bulk) or slow-release sachet. Also targets broad mites.

Thrips larvaeBroad mites

Persimilis Mite

Bright red, incredibly fast — literally outruns two-spotted spider mites. Best for active, heavy infestations. Needs humidity consistently above 60%. Combine with fallacis for longer coverage.

Two-spotted spider mite

Fallacis / Californicus Mites

Better suited for ambient humidity (40–60%). Fallacis eats 15–30 spider mites/day; californicus (~5/day) suits prevention. Both eat a broader range of pest mite species than persimilis.

Underground Allies

Soil-Dwelling Defenders

Stratiolaelaps Mites

Reddish-brown microscopic soil mites. Truly essential for overall soil health — eat thrips pupae, fungus gnat larvae, overwintering spider mites, mealybug eggs, and pathogenic fungi and bacteria. Work in soil-less media too (LECA, pon, moss). Cannot tolerate dry conditions — not suitable for cacti or succulents.

Thrips pupaeFungus gnat larvaeMealybug eggsSoil pathogens

Do not skip stratiolaelaps for thrips!

If you only treat foliage, new thrips will keep emerging from the soil and reinfecting the plant. Soil treatment is non-negotiable for full eradication.

Nematodes (Sf strain)

Microscopic parasitic roundworms that attack larvae in growing media. They live up to 18 months in soil and don't harm other beneficial insects. Best for fungus gnats and thrips pupae. Store refrigerated (unopened) for up to 2 weeks.

Fungus gnat eggsThrips eggs

Stratiolaelaps vs. Nematodes

For active infestations, start with stratiolaelaps (more voracious). Nematodes work well as a follow-up or for longer-term prevention. Stratiolaelaps also works in soil-less media; nematodes require a moist substrate.

Treatment Protocol

How to Treat an Active Infestation

1

Physically Remove Pests First

Wipe all leaves with a wet washcloth, or rinse the plant on an angle so pests drain away — not back into the pot. Repeat until your beneficials arrive.

2

Order & Release Beneficials

Select predators for each life stage of your pest (see matrix). Release lacewings first; wait 15 minutes before releasing other mites. Apply every two weeks for at least three rounds.

3

Stop Rinsing After Release

Once beneficials are on the plant, stop washing — you'll remove them. You can still spot-clean with a damp towel if you see visible pest insects.

4

Treat the Soil

Add stratiolaelaps (and nematodes for thrips or fungus gnats) at the same time as foliage treatment. Without soil treatment, pests re-emerge from the growing medium.

⚠ Avoid These Common Mistakes

  • Relying on pesticide sprays alone — many don't kill on contact and can cause resistance
  • Using systemic granules for spider mites — these increase spider mite fertility and can cause population explosions
  • Using pyrethrins on spider mites — can cause populations to "flare"
  • Baking or sterilizing new soil — this kills beneficial soil microbes your plants need
  • Only treating foliage for thrips — pupae in soil will continue reinfecting

Quantity Guidance

DMV Beneficials bases sizing estimates on a 6-inch pot as average. Larger plants need more predators; prevention requires fewer than active treatment. Their website includes graphics for each product.

Case Study

Thrips: A Multi-Stage Battle

Thrips need three predators to address every life stage. Treating one stage without the others leads to continued re-infestation.

Life Stage Target Predator
Adults & Teens Minute pirate bug (preferred) or Lacewing larvae
Young Larvae Cucumeris mites (or swirskii if >85°F)
Pupae in Soil Stratiolaelaps (first 2 rounds) then Nematodes Sf

Apply all three every two weeks · minimum three treatments

Pirate Bug vs. Lacewings?

Pirate bugs are somewhat more effective — they attack thrips even when not hungry (carrying dead thrips as trophies). They cost roughly twice as much. DMV Beneficials recommends pirate bugs for the first aggressive application, then switching to lacewings for follow-ups.

Cucumeris vs. Swirskii?

Cucumeris works better in most home conditions. Only choose swirskii if your growing temperature consistently exceeds 85°F (29°C).

Long-Term Strategy

Prevention: Staying Ahead of Pests

🌱 Soil Health

Treat your entire plant collection with nematodes Sf at least once a year as standard practice against fungus gnats and thrips. Apply stratiolaelaps mites roughly four times per year — they prevent thrips, root mealybugs, and fungus gnats, and also consume pathogenic fungi and bacteria.

Stratiolaelaps also works in soil-less media (LECA, pon, moss) — a significant advantage over nematodes.

🔬 Predatory Mites on Rotation

Select the appropriate predatory mite for your conditions and release every four months. This creates a standing population that catches infestations before they establish.

  • High humidity (>60%) → persimilis for spider mites
  • Ambient humidity → californicus or fallacis
  • Thrips + broad mites → cucumeris

🦋 Lacewing "Sweeps"

Since lacewing larvae eat a wide range of pests, releasing them regularly every 3–4 months functions like a preventive sweep across your collection. Adjust frequency based on your plant history, budget, and peace of mind.

📦 New Plant Protocol

New soil bags can harbor pest eggs. Do not sterilize new soil — this kills beneficial microbes. Instead, treat new plants and fresh potting mix with stratiolaelaps and/or nematodes before or shortly after potting. Quarantine new acquisitions for 2–4 weeks.
